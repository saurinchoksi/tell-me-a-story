Evaluation → decision

My pipeline renamed the heroes of my daughter's story.
The replacement listens instead of guessing.

An eval built on my own ears took a name-correction step from 13 confident wrong substitutions to zero, and caught two errors in my answer key along the way.

Problem

My local bedtime-story pipeline transcribes the stories my daughter and I tell out loud. When the story is a known one, a night of the Mahabharata, the by-ear transcriber mangles the names: "Bhishma" comes out Bushma, "Pandavas" comes out fondos.

The pipeline had a step to fix those. It guessed from spelling, with no idea which story it was in, and on one recording it rewrote the child's word for the heroes as Bhishma, a character on the enemy's side, through the whole war. I only knew for sure by listening back to the audio myself. That listen produced two things: the decision to remove the step, and a 36-item answer key of what each garbled name really was, marked by ear against the recording.

The constraint that shapes everything: family recordings never leave the machine, so every model involved has to be small and local.

Action

Instead of guessing better from spelling, I tested a different idea: tell Whisper which story it's in, and let it listen again. The experiments ran against the by-ear key, scored by audio timestamp so any re-transcription could be compared to what I actually heard.

The frontier, on 35 scoreable names: blind Whisper got 16. One context sentence at the start of the file got 18, and the fix decayed by minute nine; the bias only lives in the first window. Re-prompting a few seconds of audio around each suspect name, with the world's cast in the prompt, got 24. Two controls proved the gain was the cast and not the clipping: the same clips with a world-only prompt scored 15, and with no prompt 11.

Turning that into a correction stage meant an acceptance gate, and every rule in it was bought by a failure the eval caught:

Match the word at the target position, not the loudest name in the clip. First version scored a re-heard "The Kauravas did die and the Pandavas did not" as a fix for the wrong word. An ordinary word never maps onto a name. "father" sound-matches a minor character, and one hallucinated cast entry nearly pulled a real word onto a fake name. A real English word in the transcript is never overwritten automatically. The system's two remaining wrong fixes were both the word "arrows," confirmed by my ear; that class now goes to a review queue instead of the transcript. Ask for the world's groups separately. Asked for "characters," every small model I tried drops collective names like "Pandavas," which is what a child says most; a second prompt for families and factions fixes it.

What the gate can't decide goes to a one-tap review screen, and an approved fix is written into that world's dictionary, so it applies automatically every night after.

Result

On the held-out Mahabharata recording, the shipped stage made 43 corrections with 0 wrong against the by-ear key, where the removed step's 43 corrections had included 13 wrong ones. On 4 recordings it had never seen, spanning 6 stories, it correctly did nothing everywhere no world could be recognized, including telling apart, inside one recording, two invented stories from a real Thomas & Friends story.

The eval also audited its own ruler. Scoring pressure surfaced 2 data-entry errors in my answer keys, about 5% of a 36-item key, and one "wrong" auto-fix turned out to be the key's mistake, not the system's. The queue absorbed it either way: the fix waited for my tap instead of writing itself, which is the design doing its job on a human error as well as a machine one.

The decisions this eval paid for, in order: remove a shipped component, choose audio-grounded re-decoding over better text guessing, set the auto-apply line at "sound-alike, in-cast, not a real word," and keep a human on everything else.
