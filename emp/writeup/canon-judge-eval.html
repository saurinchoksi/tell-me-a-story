Local-model evaluation

I built a machine to route around a weak model.
I was fixing the wrong part.

A newer small model with a plain prompt beat the machine I’d built around an old one. Admitting that was hard. Trusting the number it gave me was harder.

Problem

My local bedtime-story pipeline transcribes the stories I tell out loud, by ear. When the story is a known one, say a night of the Mahabharata, the by-ear transcriber mangles the characters’ names. I want a step that catches those: a name from a real story spelled wrong, fixed back to the spelling the story actually uses.

The trouble is how badly the names come out. Some are near misses; one was barely a name at all:

That last one, Urzi for Arjuna, I’d written off as the floor: too degraded for anything reading text to recover. The whole job runs on-device, so family recordings never leave the machine, which rules out a big cloud model. It has to work on a small one.

Action

My first idea was the obvious one: hand a small local model the names in the transcript and ask which are misspelled characters from a known story, and what the right spelling is. On the real held-out Mahabharata recording, running Gemma-4 E4B, it caught 1 of 11. I wrote the model off as a wall and went looking for a way around it.

The way around it got elaborate. Instead of asking the model to judge, I had it generate: produce a cast list of a world’s characters, then match the transcript’s garbled names against that cast by sound and fuzzy spelling, and union several models’ lists to fill the gaps. It worked. It reached 8 of 11 on the real Mahabharata and about 0.65 across a synthetic spread of seven worlds (Star Wars, He‑Man, Mario, the Ninja Turtles, Steven Universe, Harry Potter, the Mahabharata; 40 planted misspellings). Real progress, and a lot of moving parts.

One scare kept me honest about what I was measuring. An earlier version of the machine scored 0.57 on my synthetic fixtures and 0 of 11 on the real recording. The fakes were too clean: in each one a name was only ever misspelled, never also spelled right in the same story, so the model never met the failure that actually happens. Only the held-out real recording caught the lie.

Result

Then a newer small model came out: Qwen3.5-4B, still under 5 GB, still on-device. I dropped it into the dead-end idea, unchanged: hand it the transcript names in plain text, ask which are misspelled canon and the correct spelling. No cast, no fuzzy matching, no curated list.

It scored 8 of 11 on the held-out Mahabharata, the same number the whole elaborate machine reached, in one model and one step. The three “misses” aren’t real errors: they’re the epic’s title spelled two ways and a stray phrase artifact, not characters. On the genuine character errors it went 8 for 8, with 8 of 9 flags right. It even caught Urzi → Arjuna, the name I’d declared the permanent floor.

The machine had been compensating for the weak model the whole time. Swap in a capable one and the original idea just works. The elaborate part was paying for the wrong component.

One more lift was worth taking. The judge and plain sound-matching (Double Metaphone, against a cast the same Qwen model writes) fail in different places, so I unioned their outputs. On the seven-world synthetic spread that took recall 0.72 → 0.85: two independent methods cover more ground than either alone.

1  ·  A capable model wants a simple prompt. Every dial I added to the judge made it worse: a confidence guard, few-shot examples, the cast pasted in as context. All hurt. The plain prompt won. The cleverness belongs in the model now, not in the scaffolding around it.
2  ·  Union the outputs; never feed one method into the other. Stacking only worked the independent way: combine what each method catches. When I instead fed the cast into the judge’s prompt as context, it got over-eager and precision halved. The two methods help by being separate; coupling them poisons one.
3  ·  Only the held-out real recording told the truth. The synthetic fixtures said 0.57 on a version the real recording scored 0 on, because the fakes were too clean to contain the real failure shape. Data you held back is the only data that can correct you.

The number wasn’t trustworthy yet

8 of 11 looked like a finish line. It wasn’t. I re-ran the judge and the score moved. The model hadn’t changed. I’d handed it the names in a different order.

One borderline name showed me the whole problem. Bandos, the way I’d said Pandavas, got caught in five of eight shuffles of the same list. The model runs greedy, one temperature, so it should give the same answer every time. But the order of the list tipped a name it was unsure about, on or off. Production fed it the names alphabetically, so my headline number was hostage to a sort.

So I stopped trusting one run. The judge now reads the list seven times, shuffled, and keeps the names a majority of the runs agree on. The borderline catches stop flickering. The number comes back the same every time.

Voting also surfaced what a lucky run had hidden. Told a story’s world, the model over-reaches: it maps a child’s made-up characters onto that world’s canon. On a Thomas the Tank Engine night it had never seen, it decided an invented Pataki was really Paddy, thirty times over. So I ship only the catches it can back up by sound, where the corrected spelling actually sounds like what was heard. Bishma → Bhishma stays. Pataki → Paddy goes.

Then, checking the score against my by-ear answer key, I found the ruler was bent: a correctly-spelled word marked as an error, and stale entries left from an older transcriber. Earlier, one stray clause in the live prompt, “names may be garbled,” had silently switched off the model’s world-recognition until I caught it. Both only surfaced against the real recording. When the number moves, check your answer key before you blame the model.

Making an eval trustworthy is a different job than making the number high.

On the corrected key, with the vote and the sound-check, the honest result is 5 of 9 real misspellings caught, 5 of 6 flags right, and the same on every run. It reads lower than the 8 I opened with, but part of that 8 was never real: the old key had marked a correctly-spelled word as an error, so the honest count was out of 9, not 11. The vote killed the lucky runs. The sound-check gave up exactly the wrong catches. The number didn’t get worse. I trust it more. Across the seven synthetic worlds the sound-check is the precision dial: it trades recall 0.85 → 0.75 to cut false flags from three to one, and the catches it gives up are the over-reaches.

The detector got better. Earning the right to trust the number was the work.

Built and measured locally on an Apple-silicon Mac with a sub-5 GB on-device model; nothing leaves the machine, for family privacy. The benchmark, the judge, and the scorer are on GitHub. Written with AI assistance, reviewed and edited by me.
