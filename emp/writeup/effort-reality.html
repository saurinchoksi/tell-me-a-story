Fixing the name checks — the whole effort, honestly

Two connected pieces of work. What each set out to fix, and what’s actually true now.

The pipeline is supposed to catch character names from a known story that got transcribed wrong — the Mahabharata’s Bhishma written as “Bishma.” It works in two steps: first a step names the story’s world; then, only for a known world, a check looks for the misspelled names. We worked on both steps. Here’s the reality of each.
Part 1 · the upstream step
Naming the story’s world Fixed

Problem

The step that names the world was calling real stories “made up,” which silently switched the name-check off. On a held-out Mahabharata recording it caught zero misspelled names — it had read only about a dozen scattered lines of each story and leaned on a hard-coded list of our own worlds.

Action

Built a benchmark of 100 fake bedtime stories with known answers. Tested reading the whole story against the old dozen-line sample, tuned the prompt across several versions, and cold-tested on real recordings we’d deliberately held back.

Result

Fixed, and live. It reads the whole story now and uses general knowledge instead of our hard-coded list; the held-out Mahabharata is recognized correctly. Benchmark accuracy went from about two-thirds right to about four-fifths (0.67 → 0.82). The real surprise came from the held-out test: the main cause of the miss was how little it read (the sampling), not the wording — we’d been about to rewrite the prompt and fix the wrong thing.

Part 2 · the downstream check
Catching the misspelled names Measured & right-sized

Problem

With the switch finally on, the name-check itself turned out unreliable — it flagged ordinary words (it saw “arrows” and called it a misspelling of a character named “Arrow”) — and we had never tested whether it catches the real errors.

Action

Added a filter so it stops flagging plain dictionary words. Then listened to two recordings by ear, wrote down every name the transcriber actually got wrong, and scored the check against that list.

Result

The filter works and is live — false alarms on the test recording dropped from 24 to 2. But the check itself is weak: it catches about 1 in 12 of the real misspellings. So we marked it “experimental” and chose not to hand-build a list of worlds to prop it up.

The wall behind both The small model running on the Mac simply doesn’t know enough. It can’t place less-famous worlds, and it can’t spot most misspelled names even when it does know the world. That’s a knowledge limit of a small, private, on-device model — not something cleverer wording fixes.
Bottom line We fixed the upstream switch — a real, live improvement — and we honestly measured the downstream check and found it weak, so we stopped building on it. One clear win, one honest “this doesn’t work well, good to know.” The lasting tools are the benchmark and the by-ear answer key: they’re what turned hunches into numbers we can trust.
