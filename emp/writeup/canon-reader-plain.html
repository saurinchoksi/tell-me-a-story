The canon name-check, honestly

What we set out to fix, what we did, and what’s actually true now.

Problem

We have a check that’s meant to catch character names from a known story that the transcriber spelled wrong — the Mahabharata’s Bhishma written as “Bishma.” Two things were wrong with it:

  • It flagged ordinary everyday words. It saw “arrows” and decided that was a misspelling of a character named “Arrow.”
  • We had never actually tested whether it catches the real misspellings.

Action

Two things:

Added a filter so it stops flagging plain dictionary words.

Listened to two recordings by ear, wrote down every character name the transcriber actually got wrong, and checked the detector against that list.

Result

  • The filter worked, and it’s live. The check no longer cries wolf on ordinary words — on the test recording, false alarms dropped from 24 to 2.
  • The by-ear test showed the check itself is weak. It catches about 1 in 12 of the real misspelled names. The small model running on the Mac just doesn’t reliably know these names.
  • So we stopped investing in it. Marked it “experimental,” and chose not to hand-build a list of worlds to prop it up.
Bottom line We didn’t make the detector good — we made it quiet, and we proved it’s weak. That told us to stop building on it and lean on the simpler checks instead.

Why it felt confusing: the same stretch of work did two opposite-feeling things at once — it fixed something (the noise) and found something disappointing (the detector’s weak). The real deliverable was knowing, not improving: we now know it doesn’t work well, and why, so we don’t waste more time on it.
