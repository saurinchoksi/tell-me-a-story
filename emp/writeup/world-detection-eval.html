Prompt evaluation

I blamed the prompt.
A held-out test blamed the sampling.

How a hundred fake bedtime stories caught me misdiagnosing my own bug — and fixed it anyway.

Problem

My local bedtime-story pipeline has a small step that reads each story and names the world it’s set in — Star Wars, the Mahabharata, or “made up.” That one label is a switch. Name a real world and the pipeline holds its characters to their real spellings; say “made up” and that check quietly turns off.

On a real, held-out Mahabharata recording, the step called the story “made up” — and silently caught zero of its misspelled canon names. The transcriber had heard the epic’s characters as garble:

I had two suspects. The step read only about a dozen scattered, sampled lines of each story, not the whole thing. And its prompt named my own recordings’ worlds as the examples (“Thomas & Friends,” “Mahabharata”) while flatly telling the model not to use outside knowledge. My money was on the prompt.

Action

Rather than trust the hunch, I built a benchmark to measure it: 100 fabricated bedtime stories with known answers, each written like a real transcript — rambling, spoken, with the proper names deliberately mis-heard.

The worlds spanned a deliberate range of fame — Star Wars, He‑Man, Harry Potter, Paw Patrol, Octonauts, and on down to lesser‑known ones (Hilda, Redwall, the Moomins, Earthsea, Tintin). Crucially, I held out my own real worlds (the Mahabharata and Thomas & Friends) entirely — the benchmark never saw them, so they could serve as a cold final test.

A firewall kept the eval honest: the program that ran the model saw only each story’s text, never the answer key — a separate scorer reads the answers. Then I ran an ablation (whole story vs. the old 12‑line sample), tuned the prompt across several candidates, and scored each on a confusion matrix that breaks out the two mistakes that actually cost something:

missed a world — called a real world “made up” (the switch‑off error), and invented a world — named a world for a story that was genuinely made up.

Then the cold test on the recordings the tuning never touched — and the result that reset my whole diagnosis. Even the old prompt names the Mahabharata correctly, the moment it reads the whole story.

The ablation said the same thing in numbers: feeding the whole story instead of the sample — with no change to the prompt at all — cut “missed a world” from 25 to 17. I’d been about to rewrite my way out of a problem that was mostly about how much the model got to read.

1  ·  The held-out test corrected me. The dominant cause of the original miss was the sampling, not the wording. A benchmark you tune against can’t tell you that — only data you held back can. The whole-story fix is the high-confidence win.
2  ·  The new prompt’s real value is breadth. Dropping my own worlds as examples and letting the model use its knowledge lifted accuracy 0.67 → 0.82, and — mostly — stopped it inventing worlds for made-up stories (7 → 1 false alarms). A separate win, stacked on the first.
3  ·  And a real wall, honestly bounded. The shipped prompt is reliable where the model has knowledge to lean on, and degrades exactly where it doesn’t — as the canonical names thin out, and as the world gets less famous.

Lesser-known = Hilda, Earthsea, Redwall, the Moomins, Tintin (one story each); only Hilda landed.

There’s no free lunch here. The “always commit” prompt cut misses to 5 — but then hallucinated a world onto 17 made-up stories. You can buy recall on real worlds only by paying in false alarms on invented ones.

So the shipped prompt sits at the balance point, and the remaining gap isn’t a wording problem — it’s a knowledge problem. Closing it needs a bigger model or a short reference list of the worlds I actually tell, not another rewrite.

The detector got better, but the benchmark is the real artifact. It’s what turned “I think it’s the prompt” into a number that said otherwise — and it’ll still be there, with its firewall and its held-out worlds, the next time I’m sure I know what’s broken.
